OSU Design Case

Sustaining Political Engagement
in the United States

The United States of America needs to slow down. Democracy is slow and clumsy. Consensus takes time. It is not efficient. It often doesn’t produce much beyond conversation. That’s what the founders sought. If they wanted quick and efficient, they’ve have kept their king. Yet if you ever felt inclined to speed up this form of government, silencing voices is an efficient way of doing so. And that happens when a city council refuses public comment. It happens when civic classes are removed from classrooms. It happens every election, when candidates want you to know their name but won’t engage with constituents after they’ve won. During this course, I asked how might we slow down and expand civic engagement beyond election day into every day?

In starting out, seeing this a learning experience, I hypothesized ways one might want to engage with others (learner-to-learner), their elected officials & political leaders/organizers (learner-to-facilitator), and the government processes (learner-to-content) so that one could learn how the system operates, their place within it, and how they can effect change. The end goal being to sustain a citizen’s interest over long enough time that political engagement becomes another facet of their everyday lives. Some considerable constraints include time and resources. The project is overwhelming in scope, and just like democracy itself, would only be effective if developed with as much input as possible. So, I decided to focus on building as much empathy as possible between the design team and the populace. I went about developing a role-playing prototype so that designers can wear as many hats as possible in various scenarios that may occur when experiencing the end product. This would take place after a long discovery phase of conducting interviews and field studies to produce various user personas to use during the role-playing prototype. I also invision this happening before approaching the project’s UI. Throughout this course, I continually asked myself, “Why would anyone trust this experience?” Trust seems to be at an all-time low in American politics. So, the focus of these role-play prototypes would be to create a first impression for new users that exudes trust.

In these role-play exercises, one designer would play a user persona in various scenarios. Both user persona and scenario used in each session would be displayed on cue cards during the session. This designer would speak aloud their inner thoughts about what they’re experiencing. Another designer would play the end product’s interface and react as best as they could to create trust. A third designer would observe and take notes. Before and after each session are built-in reflection breaks to capture how designers thought the role-play session went and why. Key features include being able to rapidly combine different user personas and scenarios for trying out various combinations that could occur. Another is not just to empathize with people from/in different backgrounds and settings, but for the designers to reflect on the goals they’re trying to achieve. It isn’t enough to use this moment to ask if they’re designing a comfortable experience for the users. The designers could get swept up with trying to build a trusting, comforting experience, and lose the overarching goal. And so, a common question during reflection will be for designers to ask, “During this session, did you solve the problem correctly (build trust with the user)? Did you solve the right problem correctly (build trust without the expense of moving away from the overarching goal)?” From this design, the more I explored the idea of this role-playing prototype, the more I realized how difficult it seems to approach the UI without first understanding for whom I am designing the interface. It made me more aware of the need to avoid jumping to conclusions in design. During this course, all of my time dealing with this prototype was theoretical. Next time, I’d like to test the prototype out with others to see how successful it could actually be.
