Online Course Template for Exploring Purpose in General Education Courses

Challenge

How might we afford higher education learners and faculty the opportunity to construct their own meaning and find purpose within a required general education (gen ed) course?

Process

For the capstone project of my LXD certification with Oregon State University, I set out to design a template for higher education faculty and designers who are developing general education courses. This template’s purpose would not be to try to customize the content to the learner, but to allow learners and faculty to construct their own meaning and use it throughout the course so that the content remains relevant to them.

  1. During my undergraduate career and then working as an eLearning multimedia designer at a community college, I've seen that many learners in required general education courses are not sure the coursework applies to them. They do the work because they must, but not becuase it is their interest. Rather than keeping the current frame of these courses—a one-to-many relationship where the course attempts to explain why the subject is important—I sought to reframe the course as a many-to-one relationship, where each person applies their own meaning to the course.

  2. After conducting interviews with undergraduate learners, faculty, and other designers, I decided to focus on a common part of every online course: the Start Here module. Colleges tend to use this module to explain important information about the course, but it not very exciting or interactive. I wanted to reframe this aspect of the course from simply telling the learner what is important, to providing learners the opportunity to reflect on what the course means to them and work in small groups to customize the course, where possible, to have the course better fit their constructed meaning.

  3. The Start Here module would end with a democratic discussion and vote where all learners would be able to decide, with the instructor/facilitator, how they could customize the course. The more important aspect of this is allowing all learners to the chance to discover their own meaning, regardless of how the course is customized, and to hear how others view the course.

  4. In addition, I also redesigned another common aspect of online courses, the Instructor Resources module, to be interactive for the faculty member teaching the course. The goal here was to provide faculty the opportunity to reflect on how this course (and learners’ perception of it) changes what they think about their own field of study.

  5. Finally, throughout the course, I created small group discussions and whole-class discussions to check in about what is and isn’t working. This gives learners and faculty the chance to act as co-designers of the course rather than passive consumers within an online course.
